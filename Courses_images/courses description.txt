Tamang Selo Dance Course
Tamang Selo is a lively folk dance and music tradition of the Tamang community, often called the heartbeat of the hills. It is performed to fast, driving rhythms on the damphu drum and accompanied by playful, expressive songs that tell stories of love, daily life, and the mountains. In villages and towns, Tamang Selo brings people together during festivals and celebrations, creating a joyful circle of movement and sound. The dance style is energetic but friendly, making it welcoming even for beginners.
​

In this course, students are introduced to the cultural background of the Tamang people and the role of Tamang Selo in their social life. Step by step, learners practice basic footwork patterns, hip movements, claps, and turns that match the damphu rhythms. The instructor guides group formations, call‑and‑response gestures, and simple choreographies that can be performed in lines or circles.
​

Classes also explore costume elements, music structure, and performance manners to build confidence for stage or community events. By the end, students can perform a complete Tamang Selo routine with correct timing and expression, while understanding the stories behind the songs. This course is ideal for beginners, folk dance lovers, and anyone wanting to experience an uplifting, hill‑region dance of Nepal.

Murchunga Jaw Harp Course
The murchunga (jaw harp) is a small metal instrument held in the mouth and plucked to produce twanging, rhythmic tones. It appears in various Himalayan and global folk traditions and can create hypnotic, drone‑like patterns. In Nepal, it is used for personal enjoyment, folk music, and modern fusion.
​

In this course, students learn how to position the murchunga, control mouth shape, and pluck safely. The instructor guides learners through simple rhythmic patterns, exploring how changing the oral cavity shapes the sound. Basic grooves and beat‑based playing are practiced first.
​

The course encourages creativity and careful listening to overtones and resonances. By completion, students can play short pieces and add unique textures to folk or fusion music. This course is ideal for experimental musicians, folk enthusiasts, and those who enjoy compact, special‑effect instruments.
​

Khainjadi Percussion Course
Khainjadi is a type of small frame or hand drum used in devotional and folk contexts in Nepal. It often accompanies bhajans (devotional songs) and light folk performances, providing gentle but clear rhythm. Its portable size makes it popular in temples and informal gatherings.
​

In this course, students learn to hold and play khainjadi using fingers and palm. The instructor introduces basic stroke patterns, simple tala cycles, and ways to support singing without overpowering it. Learners practice keeping steady tempo and adding tasteful accents.
​

The course also explains how khainjadi fits into bhajan groups and small ensembles. By the end, participants can accompany simple devotional and folk songs comfortably. This course suits singers, spiritual practitioners, and anyone wanting to add soft percussion to their music.
​

Pungi/Bin Snake Charm Course
The pungi or bin is a reed wind instrument traditionally associated with snake charmers in South Asia. It produces a reedy, continuous sound when blown using circular breathing, often with fast, looping melodies. While the traditional snake‑charming context is changing, the instrument remains symbolically powerful and musically interesting.
​

In this course, students are introduced to basic pungi playing using ethical, performance‑oriented approaches. The instructor teaches how to blow steadily, finger simple melodies, and understand the scale of the instrument. Circular breathing may be introduced gradually for advanced students.
​

The course focuses on musical expression rather than animal performance, linking the instrument to folk and fusion contexts. By completion, learners can play simple tunes that evoke the classic bin sound. This course is for curious wind‑instrument players and performers wanting an unusual, traditional timbre in their music.
​

Yalamber String Course
Yalamber is referenced as a traditional string instrument name connected to regional musical heritage. Instruments of this kind typically have a rustic construction and are used in folk singing and storytelling. Their sound adds a distinctive color to local ensembles.
​

In this course, students learn the basics of holding, tuning, and plucking a Yalamber‑type string instrument. The instructor introduces simple scale patterns, chords or drones, and basic accompaniment techniques for folk songs. Learners practice steady rhythm and tone production.
​

The course also covers the historical or legendary associations linked to the name Yalamber in Nepali culture. By completion, students can accompany at least a few folk songs and understand the place of such instruments in regional music. This course is ideal for folk music fans seeking lesser‑known traditional strings.
​

Ektare Frame Drum Course
Ektare/ektara can refer to a single‑stringed instrument, and in some contexts, a simple drum setup is used in folk traditions. Frame drums with one playing surface are common worldwide in devotional and folk music, including in South Asia. They provide straightforward yet powerful rhythm support.
​

In this course, students learn basic frame‑drum technique suitable for Nepali folk and devotional contexts. The instructor demonstrates hand positions, strokes, and simple rhythmic patterns. Learners practice supporting singing and dance at different tempos.
​

The course emphasizes listening, dynamics, and endurance for longer sessions. By the end, participants can accompany bhajans, folk songs, and circle dances with steady beats. This course suits percussion beginners and singers who want an easy‑to‑carry rhythm instrument.
​

Thangka Painting Course
Thangka painting is a sacred Buddhist art form that depicts deities, mandalas, and religious scenes on cotton or silk using mineral pigments and fine brushwork. Originating in Tibetan and Himalayan regions, thangkas serve as meditation aids and teaching tools rather than simple decoration. In Nepal, Newar and Tibetan artists have developed highly refined styles.

In this course, students are introduced to the spiritual and artistic foundations of thangka painting. The instructor explains canvas preparation, grid drawing, and traditional iconometric proportions used for deities. Learners practice basic line drawing, filling flat colors, and gradually progressing to shading and details. Use of gold and mineral pigments is demonstrated where appropriate.
​

The course stresses patience, discipline, and respect for the sacred subjects. By completion, students may finish a simple thangka motif or small deity image while understanding its symbolism. This course is ideal for serious art students and spiritual practitioners who want to connect with Himalayan Buddhist visual tradition.

Newari Woodcarving Course
Newari woodcarving is renowned worldwide for its intricate designs on temple doors, windows, pillars, and household items in the Kathmandu Valley. Motifs include deities, flowers, geometrical patterns, and mythological scenes carved into hardwoods. This craft is central to the valley’s architectural identity.
​

In this course, students learn basic Newari carving techniques from design to finishing. The instructor introduces tools, wood selection, and safety, then guides learners through drawing simple patterns on wood blocks. Gradually, students practice carving lines, curves, and relief designs, moving toward traditional motifs.
​

The course emphasizes precision, patience, and appreciation of cultural symbolism in each design. By the end, participants may complete small carved panels or decorative objects inspired by Newar architecture. This course is ideal for craft lovers and aspiring artisans interested in Nepal’s historic woodwork tradition.
​

Pottery Making Course
Pottery is an ancient craft in Nepal, visible in traditional pottery squares like those in Bhaktapur, where artisans shape clay vessels by hand and wheel. Clay pots have been used for cooking, storage, rituals, and decoration for generations. The craft combines practical function with artistic creativity.
​

In this course, students learn fundamental pottery techniques from clay preparation to firing. The instructor teaches hand‑building methods (pinching, coiling) and demonstrates basic wheel throwing for bowls or cups. Learners practice forming, trimming, and decorating surfaces with simple textures or slips.
​

The course also introduces local pottery traditions and the role of potters in community life. By completion, students can create small functional or decorative pieces, gaining a hands‑on understanding of this earthy, grounding art. This course suits anyone who enjoys working with natural materials and traditional crafts.
​

Carpet Weaving Course
Hand‑woven carpets are a significant traditional craft in Nepal, influenced by Tibetan and local designs. They often feature geometric patterns, symbolic motifs, and rich colors, serving both practical and artistic purposes. Carpet weaving supports many artisan families and export industries.
​

In this course, learners are introduced to the basics of carpet weaving on a loom. The instructor explains warp and weft, knotting techniques, and pattern reading. Students start with small sample pieces to practice tension control, color changes, and finishing edges.
​

The course discusses traditional motifs and the social role of weaving communities. By the end, participants may complete a small mat or wall hanging that reflects Himalayan design. This course is ideal for textile lovers and anyone interested in slow, meditative craft processes.
​

Himalayan Paper Craft Course
Traditional Himalayan paper, often made from lokta bark, is valued for its strength, durability, and natural texture. It is used for manuscripts, prayer flags, notebooks, lamps, and decorative items. Paper‑making and craft support rural livelihoods and eco‑friendly production.
​

In this course, students explore both lokta paper history and creative applications. The instructor demonstrates sheet formation (where possible) and then teaches folding, cutting, and assembling techniques to make cards, envelopes, lanterns, and small artworks. Learners experiment with natural dyes and simple prints.
​

The course emphasizes sustainability and respect for traditional materials. By completion, students can design and craft their own paper products with a Himalayan touch. This course suits crafters, designers, and eco‑conscious creatives looking for unique textures and stories in their work.
​

Tibetan Handicrafts Course
Tibetan handicrafts in Nepal include items like prayer wheels, incense burners, jewelry, textiles, and ritual objects made by Tibetan and Himalayan artisans. These crafts blend function, symbolism, and aesthetic beauty, often linked to Buddhist practice. Many are produced in refugee settlements and craft centers.

In this course, students are introduced to a selection of Tibetan craft techniques suitable for beginners. Depending on focus, the instructor may cover simple metal embossing, bead jewelry, fabric appliqué, or small ritual object decoration. Learners gain hands‑on experience with traditional motifs such as endless knots, lotus flowers, and auspicious symbols.
​

The course also explains the cultural and spiritual meaning behind common designs and objects. By the end, participants create a few small handcrafted items that reflect Tibetan artistic sensibility. This course suits craft lovers and spiritual seekers interested in Himalayan Buddhist material culture.
​

Buddhist Statue Carving Course
Buddhist statue carving in Nepal, especially by Newar and Tibetan artisans, is a highly skilled craft involving metal, stone, or wood. Statues depict Buddhas, bodhisattvas, and protective deities with precise iconographic rules. The work combines devotional intention with fine technical mastery.
​

In this course, students receive an introductory, simplified approach to statue carving and modeling. The instructor explains proportions and basic iconography for simple Buddha images, then guides learners through working with clay or soft material before any harder medium. Techniques include rough shaping, refining forms, and detailing basic features.
​

The course emphasizes respect for sacred imagery and careful, mindful practice. By completion, learners may produce small practice figures that help them understand the complexity of professional statues. This course is suitable for serious art students and practitioners drawn to Buddhist sacred art.
​

Mithila Wall Art Course
Mithila or Maithili art is a traditional painting style from the Mithila region, including parts of Nepal’s Terai such as Dhanusha. Historically, women painted vivid designs on walls and floors during rituals and special occasions, depicting gods, animals, and symbolic patterns. Today, Mithila art also appears on paper, canvas, and textiles.
​

In this course, students learn the characteristic motifs, line work, and color schemes of Mithila painting. The instructor demonstrates how to sketch and fill figures like fish, peacocks, lotus flowers, and deities using flat, bright colors and strong outlines. Learners practice on paper or board, building confidence before attempting wall‑style compositions.
​

The course also explores the cultural stories behind common themes, including the famous link to the Ram–Sita marriage legend. By the end, participants can create their own Mithila‑inspired art pieces suitable for decoration or display. This course is ideal for painters and designers who love bold, narrative folk styles.
​

Khukuri Knife Forging Course
The khukuri is Nepal’s iconic curved knife, closely associated with Gurkha soldiers and Himalayan identity. Traditional khukuris are hand‑forged by skilled smiths, combining functional strength with elegant shape and sometimes decorative engraving. The craft involves knowledge of metal, heat, and precise hammer work.
​

In this course, students receive a supervised introduction to khukuri‑style forging using safe, controlled methods. The instructor explains metal selection, heating, hammering, and basic blade shaping. Learners may practice on small practice blades or miniatures, focusing on technique rather than full combat blades.
​

The course stresses safety, respect for tools, and appreciation of the khukuri’s cultural significance. By completion, participants gain insight into blacksmithing and the effort behind a traditional khukuri. This course suits craft enthusiasts and history lovers interested in metalwork and Nepali martial heritage.
​

Bamboo Knitting Course
Bamboo work, including basketry and knitting‑like weaving, is a common rural craft across Nepal. Artisans split and shape bamboo into strips and then weave them into baskets, mats, containers, and decorative items. The craft is eco‑friendly and closely linked to daily village life.
​

In this course, students learn basic bamboo preparation and weaving/“knitting” techniques. The instructor demonstrates how to safely split bamboo, smooth strips, and start simple weaving patterns. Learners practice making small items such as coasters, pen holders, or mini baskets.
​

The course emphasizes sustainability, hand skills, and traditional design principles. By the end, participants can complete simple bamboo crafts and understand how larger baskets and functional objects are built. This course is ideal for people who enjoy natural materials and want to connect with rural Nepali craftsmanship.
